About me 02 / 07

Life is
complex — I want
to understand it

Biology always felt fascinating to me because of how incredibly complex life is. The idea that an entire organism — our behaviour, functions, strengths, vulnerabilities, even parts of our potential — can emerge from information encoded in the genome made me want to understand how living systems truly work. I was especially drawn to the question of how small dysfunctions in these systems can completely change human health, and how we might one day predict, prevent, or solve them.

Biology alone was not enough for the kind of questions I wanted to ask. The scale and complexity of living systems require computational approaches capable of connecting signals that are impossible to see manually.

That realization led me from biology into omics and bioinformatics — and then into AI. Biological systems are deeply non-linear, yet many traditional approaches simplify them into linear assumptions that work only partially. I see AI not as a replacement for biology or experimentation, but as a way to explore what is usually ignored: weak signals, hidden relationships, rare patterns, and observations often dismissed as outliers because we do not fully understand them yet.

What drives me most is not simply applying existing methods to datasets, but building tools and models designed around specific biological questions. That drive took me through a PhD at INRAe / Paris Saclay, research at the Roslin Institute in Edinburgh and Inserm in Marseille, two publications, and three international conference presentations — and it is what I am channelling today into OmicSage.
