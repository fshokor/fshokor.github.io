About me 02 / 07

Life is
complex — I want
to understand it

Biology always felt fascinating to me because of how incredibly complex life is. The idea that an entire organism — our behaviour, functions, strengths, vulnerabilities, even parts of our potential — can emerge from information encoded in the genome made me want to understand how living systems truly work. I was especially drawn to the question of how small dysfunctions in these systems can completely change human health, and how we might one day predict, prevent, or solve them.

Biology alone was not enough for the kind of questions I wanted to ask. The scale and complexity of living systems require computational approaches capable of connecting signals that are impossible to see manually.

That realization led me from biology into omics and bioinformatics — and then into AI. Biological systems are deeply non-linear, yet many traditional approaches simplify them into linear assumptions that work only partially. I see AI not as a replacement for biology or experimentation, but as a way to explore what is usually ignored: weak signals, hidden relationships, rare patterns, and observations often dismissed as outliers because we do not fully understand them yet.

What drives me most is not simply applying existing methods to datasets, but building tools and models designed around specific biological questions. That drive took me through a PhD at INRAe / Paris Saclay, research at the Roslin Institute in Edinburgh and Inserm in Marseille, two publications, and three international conference presentations — and it is what I am channelling today into OmicSage.

OmicSage

AI-assisted single-cell multi-omics analysis platform — for biologists and bioinformaticians.

GitHub

Single-cell analysis is fragmented, inaccessible, and full of repetition. No single tool covers scRNA-seq, scATAC-seq, spatial transcriptomics, and multiome integration in one place — and none of them produce automated, publication-ready reports. OmicSage solves all three: an end-to-end pipeline that works for biologists through a no-code interface and for bioinformaticians through a CLI and project management system — with AI-powered interpretation that can be turned off entirely, leaving the full pipeline running without any API key.

DLGBLUP

Hybrid deep learning + GBLUP model for nonlinear multi-trait genomic prediction.

Traditional GBLUP predicts genetic values by assuming linear relationships between traits — an assumption that rarely holds in complex biological systems. DLGBLUP adds a deep learning layer on top of multi-trait GBLUP outputs, learning to capture the nonlinear genetic correlations that statistical models miss. The result: improved genomic prediction accuracy for correlated traits, with the interpretability of GBLUP preserved as the foundation.

scRNAseq_HCC

Single-cell RNA-seq + graph neural networks for drug target discovery in liver cancer.

Bulk RNA-seq cannot capture the cellular heterogeneity of hepatocellular carcinoma — one of the most aggressive and treatment-resistant cancers. This pipeline combines scRNA-seq analysis with graph neural network-based drug ranking: QC, clustering, and four-way cell-type annotation feed into protein-protein interaction network analysis and hub gene ranking, which then drives GCN/GAT/GraphSAGE training to prioritise drug candidates. Each of the three notebooks produces a standalone HTML report.

01 PhD Thesis

2025 Thesis

Contribution of Deep Learning Approaches to Genomic Prediction and Support for Selection Objectives

Université Paris-Saclay · INRAe / Eliance

Fatima Shokor

This PhD thesis investigates how deep learning can advance genomic prediction in animal breeding — an area long dominated by linear statistical models. Across three years at INRAe and Paris-Saclay, the work develops and evaluates neural network architectures capable of capturing nonlinear genetic relationships between traits, modelling heterosis in crossbreeding systems, and supporting selection objectives that traditional methods cannot address. The thesis bridges quantitative genetics and deep learning, validating approaches on both simulated data and real livestock datasets.

02 Publications

2025 Journal

Deep learning and genomic best linear unbiased prediction integration: An approach to identify potential nonlinear genetic relationships between traits

Journal of Dairy Science · Vol. 108, No. 6

F. Shokor, P. Croiseau, H. Gangloff, R. Saintilan, T. Tribout, T. Mary-Huard, B.C.D. Cuyabano

Most genomic prediction methods assume linear relationships between traits — an assumption that often fails in complex biological systems. This paper introduces DLGBLUP, a hybrid model that first runs standard multi-trait GBLUP, then feeds its outputs into a deep learning layer that detects and exploits nonlinear genetic correlations. Validated on simulated and real dairy cattle data, DLGBLUP improves prediction accuracy for correlated traits while preserving the interpretability of the GBLUP foundation.

doi: 10.3168/jds.2024-26057 Code
2025 Preprint

Deep Learning for genomic prediction accounting for heterosis in crossbreeding systems

bioRxiv

F. Shokor, P. Croiseau, S. Fritz, A.A.A. Martin

Crossbreeding exploits hybrid vigour (heterosis) to boost animal performance, but most genomic prediction models are built for purebred animals and struggle to account for these non-additive effects. This study evaluates deep learning strategies for crossbred genomic prediction — testing how to incorporate breed composition, breed-of-origin of alleles, and explicit heterosis modelling. The key finding: DL outperforms traditional statistical methods when heterosis is present, and separating additive and heterosis predictions then combining them with effect-specific weights yields the highest accuracy.

doi: 10.1101/2025.07.24.666530 Code
